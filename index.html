Wave Physics — What to Say
Amplitude
"Amplitude is the maximum displacement of a wave from equilibrium. In deep ocean, tsunamis have amplitudes of just 0.3–1m — completely undetectable by ships. Near shore, energy conservation causes amplitude to grow to 10–40m."
Wavelength (λ)
"Tsunami wavelengths can reach 200–500 km. This is why they are so destructive — the entire wave column moves when the seafloor rises. Wind waves have wavelengths of just 100m."
Wave Equation: v = fλ
"The wave equation connects speed, frequency, and wavelength. For tsunamis: speed also equals √(g×d). At depth 4000m, v = √(9.81×4000) = 198 m/s = 713 km/h. At depth 10,000m, v = √(9.81×10000) = 313 m/s = 1,128 km/h."
Transverse vs Longitudinal
"Seismic P-waves are longitudinal — particles compress and expand parallel to the wave's direction of travel. That's why they travel fastest through solid rock. Seismic S-waves and tsunami surface waves are transverse. P-waves always arrive first at a seismograph."
Energy Transfer
"Waves transfer energy without permanently moving matter. Ocean buoys prove this — they bob up and down as a tsunami passes without being carried away. The ENERGY travels at 1000 km/h; the water particles barely move horizontally."
Why the Hybrid System Works
⚡ Problem with Seismic Only
Not every underwater earthquake generates a tsunami. Seismographs would trigger false alarms every time there is a magnitude 6+ earthquake — and coastal populations would stop trusting warnings (cry-wolf effect). The 1946 Hilo false alarm devastated public trust.
🌊 Problem with Buoys Only
Ocean buoy (DART) confirmation is highly accurate — it directly measures pressure change from a passing tsunami. However, the wave must physically travel from the epicentre to the buoy before detection. This destroys the warning advantage. The 2011 Japan warning was too slow because buoy data took too long.
✓ Hybrid Solution
Seismic sensors detect the earthquake IMMEDIATELY. This gives a preliminary alert while the buoy data is awaited. When the buoy CONFIRMS a tsunami wave exists, a full evacuation order is issued. Speed of seismic detection + accuracy of buoy confirmation = maximum warning time with minimum false alarms.

Expected Teacher Questions
Q: What formula calculates tsunami speed?
A: "v = √(g × d), where g = 9.81 m/s² and d = ocean depth in metres. At 4000m depth, that gives 198 m/s or about 713 km/h. The formula comes from shallow-water wave theory."
Q: Why do seismic waves travel so much faster?
A: "Rock is far more rigid and elastic than water. Seismic P-waves are longitudinal compression waves — the Earth's crust transmits them at 5–14 km/s depending on rock density. Water simply can't support these waves the same way."
Q: What is the difference between P and S waves?
A: "P-waves are Primary/compressional longitudinal waves — particles vibrate parallel to travel direction. They travel fastest, ~14,000 km/h. S-waves are Secondary/shear transverse waves — particles vibrate perpendicular. They travel ~8,000 km/h and can't travel through liquids."
Q: How does a DART buoy work?
A: "A DART buoy has a Bottom Pressure Recorder on the seafloor that measures changes in water pressure. A passing tsunami increases pressure by just 1–2 Pa per metre of wave height. The BPR transmits data acoustically to a surface buoy, which sends it via satellite to monitoring centres."
Q: Can you explain v = fλ?
A: "Wave speed equals frequency times wavelength. If a tsunami has a frequency of 0.0005 Hz — one wave every 33 minutes — and a wavelength of 200,000 m, then v = 0.0005 × 200,000 = 100 m/s. The interactive slider on our Wave Equation page shows this relationship visually."
Q: Is Wavara a real place?
A: "Wavara is a fictional island nation designed to represent Pacific coastal communities. However, all physics, formulas, speeds, and detection methods are based on real systems — specifically NOAA's DART II network and the Pacific Tsunami Warning Centre."
Advanced Questions
Q: Why doesn't tsunami speed equal seismic speed?
A: "Seismic waves travel through the solid elastic earth. Tsunamis travel through ocean water. The medium determines wave speed. Water is fluid — it can't support the same elastic stress as rock. The tsunami wave is also gravitational — it's driven by gravity restoring equilibrium after the seabed displacement."
Q: What happens to wave speed near the coast?
A: "As the tsunami enters shallow water, v = √(g×d) means speed decreases dramatically. But energy is conserved — since E ∝ A²v, as v drops, amplitude A must increase. This is shoaling. A 0.5m deep-ocean wave becomes a 15m coastal wave. It's also why the wavelength compresses and waves stack up."
Q: How does the hybrid system reduce false alarms?
A: "Seismic sensors alone would alert for every M6+ earthquake — but only ~1 in 10 large earthquakes generates a destructive tsunami. Requiring buoy confirmation creates a two-factor authentication system. Both must agree. This reduces false alarms by ~85% while preserving the speed advantage of seismic detection."
Q: What improvements could be made?
A: "AI pattern recognition to predict tsunami generation probability from seismic data alone. Seafloor GPS sensors to directly measure seabed uplift (coseismic deformation). Dense buoy networks to triangulate wave direction. Real-time ocean modelling to predict coastal run-up height and inundation zones."
