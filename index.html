Wave Physics Talking Points
Amplitude
"Amplitude is the maximum displacement of a wave from its equilibrium position. For tsunamis in the open ocean, amplitude may only be 1 metre — but as the wave enters shallow water, it can grow to 30+ metres due to energy conservation."
Wavelength (λ)
"Wavelength is the distance between two consecutive crests or troughs. Tsunami wavelengths can be 200 km — that's why they're almost undetectable in the deep ocean and arrive as multiple massive waves."
Frequency (f)
"Frequency is the number of wave cycles per second, measured in Hertz. The wave equation connects them: v = fλ. So if speed is constant, higher frequency means shorter wavelength — you can see this on the wave equation slider."
Energy Transfer
"Waves transfer energy without permanently moving matter. The water particles move in circles — they don't travel with the wave. This is why a buoy bobs in place when a tsunami passes. The energy travels, not the water."
Simulation Science Explained
Why Seismic Waves Arrive First
"Seismic P-waves travel through solid rock at 5–8 km/s (18,000–29,000 km/h). Tsunami waves travel through water at ~700 km/h. Rock is much denser and more elastic than water, so it transmits vibrations much faster. Seismic sensors can detect an earthquake 10–15 minutes before the tsunami even forms properly."
Why Buoys Can't Detect Instantly
"A pressure buoy measures changes in water pressure at the ocean floor. The tsunami wave must physically travel to the buoy's location before detection occurs. This can take 15–30 minutes depending on distance. However, buoys CONFIRM that a tsunami actually exists — reducing false alarms."
Why Hybrid Systems Are Better
"The hybrid system uses seismic sensors to detect the earthquake IMMEDIATELY and issue an early warning, while buoy confirmation prevents false alarms. If seismic data says 'earthquake occurred' and buoy data confirms 'wave detected', both must agree before a full evacuation order is issued. This gives more time AND more confidence."
Transverse vs Longitudinal
"Seismic P-waves are longitudinal — particles compress and expand parallel to wave motion. Seismic S-waves are transverse — particles move perpendicular. Tsunami surface waves are transverse. P-waves travel faster than S-waves, which is why seismographs receive two separate signals from one earthquake."

Teacher Questions & Answers
Q: "What formula is used for tsunami speed?"
A: "v = √(g × d), where g is gravitational acceleration (9.81 m/s²) and d is ocean depth in metres. This is real physics used by actual tsunami warning systems."
Q: "Why do seismic waves travel faster?"
A: "Rock is much more rigid and elastic than water. Seismic P-waves travel at 5–8 km/s through the earth's crust — about 25 times faster than tsunami waves through water."
Q: "What is the wave equation?"
A: "v = f × λ. Wave speed equals frequency multiplied by wavelength. You can see this demonstrated live on the Wave Equation section — moving the sliders changes the wave's appearance and instantly recalculates the speed."
Q: "What's the difference between transverse and longitudinal waves?"
A: "Transverse waves: particles move perpendicular to wave direction — like water surface waves, light, and seismic S-waves. Longitudinal waves: particles move parallel to wave direction — like sound and seismic P-waves. Both types are shown in the Wave Science section."
Q: "How does this system reduce false alarms?"
A: "The seismic sensor detects the earthquake and issues a preliminary alert. The buoy must then CONFIRM that a tsunami wave actually formed. Not every underwater earthquake generates a tsunami — by requiring confirmation from both systems, false alarms are reduced significantly."
Q: "Is Wavara a real place?"
A: "Wavara is a fictional island nation created for this project to represent coastal communities at tsunami risk. The physics, formulas, and detection methods are all based on real systems — particularly NOAA's DART II deep-ocean assessment and reporting of tsunamis."

⚠️
Public Tsunami Safety Guide
If you receive a tsunami warning, you have minutes to act. Every second counts. This guide uses the same science from WaveGuard's hybrid detection system to help you survive.
📻
Step 1
Hear the Alert
Listen for official warnings via radio, TV, sirens, or mobile alerts. Do not wait to see the wave.
🏃
Step 2
Move Immediately
Go to high ground or inland. Move at least 1 mile from the coast or to a height of 100 feet above sea level.
🚗
Step 3
Follow Evacuation Routes
Use designated evacuation routes. Avoid bridges and low-lying roads near water.
📱
Step 4
Stay Informed
Monitor official emergency channels. A tsunami can have multiple waves — the first may not be the largest.
⏳
Step 5
Wait for All-Clear
Do NOT return until authorities give the official all-clear. The threat can last several hours.
🤝
Step 6
Help Others
Help neighbours, especially elderly or disabled people. Alert others if you can do so safely.
